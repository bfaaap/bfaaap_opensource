\begin{abstract}
The piano sustain pedal is operated by foot, which excludes people who cannot
readily use their feet---players with limb disabilities, small children whose
feet do not reach the pedals, and some elderly players. We present
\textbf{bFaaaP} (\emph{barrier-Free assist as a Pedal}), a hands-free system that
controls a piano pedal from the player's \emph{head angle}. A smartphone tracks
the head pose with on-device augmented-reality face tracking and transmits a
compact control signal over Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) to a pedal device. The
signal is generated from two parameters---a head-angle \emph{threshold} and the
press \emph{speed/depth}---giving intuitive, expressive sustain control. Two
device realizations share a single controller: \emph{Pro}, a robotic actuator
that physically presses an acoustic piano's sustain pedal and is anchored by a
novel pneumatic ``airback'' that absorbs the actuation reaction force without
modifying the instrument; and \emph{Switch}, an electronic actuator that drives
the sustain input of digital pianos and keyboards. A key implementation insight
is the deliberate \emph{decoupling} of the fast face-tracking sampling rate from
the slower BLE transmission rate, which keeps the wireless link reliable. The
system has been demonstrated in public concerts and recitals from 2018 to 2026.
We release the full hardware and software as open source to support reproduction
and inclusive music-making.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} assistive technology; human--computer interaction;
accessibility; robotic actuation; head-gesture interface; music technology;
open hardware.

\section{Introduction}
Expressive piano performance relies heavily on the \emph{sustain (damper) pedal},
which is operated by the foot. This foot requirement excludes a population that
\emph{can} play the keys but cannot operate the pedal: wheelchair users and others
with lower-limb impairments, small children whose feet do not yet reach the
pedals, and some elderly players. The project began in 2018 from exactly such a
request: a wheelchair-using client asked whether the piano pedal could be
motorized so it could be ``pressed.''

We present bFaaaP, a system that lets a player operate the pedal \emph{hands-free}
using small head movements. The contributions of this paper are:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item A \textbf{two-parameter head-angle control method} (an angle threshold
  plus a press speed/depth gain, with hysteresis) that maps a natural head tilt
  to expressive pedal action (the underlying method is patented).
  \item A \textbf{rate-decoupling design} that reconciles the high frame rate of
  smartphone face tracking with the limited throughput of a BLE link, eliminating
  a class of communication faults.
  \item Two device realizations from one controller: a \textbf{robotic actuator
  for acoustic pianos} with a \textbf{pneumatic ``airback'' reaction-force
  anchor} that requires no modification of the instrument, and an
  \textbf{electronic actuator for digital pianos/keyboards}.
  \item A \textbf{sensorless mechanical-limit auto-calibration} that finds the
  pedal travel limits from motor-current (reaction-force) sensing and a user
  slider.
  \item A field-deployed, \textbf{open-source} assistive instrument, demonstrated
  in public performances over 2018--2026.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related work}
bFaaaP sits at the intersection of three areas.
\textbf{Accessible/assistive musical instruments} adapt or design instruments so
that people with motor disabilities can perform~\cite{ramirez2023,duarte2023}.
\textbf{Hands-free, head-based human--computer interfaces} use head movement (and
gaze) as an accessible input for users with limited hand use~\cite{sumak2019};
commodity smartphone augmented-reality frameworks now provide robust on-device
face/head pose estimation~\cite{arkit}.
\textbf{Robotic and mechatronic actuation of acoustic instruments} has been
studied for expressive, automated, and robotic music performance, including the
piano~\cite{scimeca2020,weinberg2020,zappi2012}. bFaaaP is distinguished by
(i)~targeting the \emph{sustain pedal} specifically, (ii)~using a \emph{commodity
smartphone} as the head sensor, and (iii)~enabling \emph{non-destructive}
placement on an \emph{unmodified} acoustic piano via pneumatic anchoring.

bFaaaP comprises two components linked by BLE (Figures~\ref{fig:overview}
and~\ref{fig:arch}):
a \textbf{smartphone controller} and a \textbf{pedal device}. The smartphone
(iOS; an iPhone/iPad with a TrueDepth front camera) tracks the player's head
pitch with AR face tracking and computes a control value. It transmits this value
to the pedal device over a Nordic UART Service (NUS) BLE link. The device then
operates the pedal so a note sustains while the head is tilted past a threshold,
and releases when the head returns.

\centering

\caption{System architecture. One smartphone controller drives two device
realizations (Switch: electronic; Pro: robotic).}
\label{fig:arch}
\end{figure}

The same controller supports two product lines: \textbf{Pro} for acoustic pianos
(grand and upright), where a motor physically depresses the sustain pedal; and
\textbf{Switch} for electric pianos and keyboards, where the sustain is actuated
electronically through the instrument's pedal jack.

\section{Head-angle control method}
\label{sec:control}
Let $\theta$ be the (downward) head pitch reported by the face tracker, $\theta_0$
a user-calibrated neutral offset, and $g$ a user gain (``speed/depth'').
The transmitted pedal value is
\begin{equation}
v = \mathrm{clamp}\big(\,g\cdot(\theta-\theta_0),\;0,\;99\,\big),
\end{equation}
encoded as a two-digit code and streamed at a fixed cadence. The discrete
engage/release events use \emph{hysteresis}: engage when
$\theta-\theta_0 \ge \tau$ and disengaged, release when
$\theta-\theta_0 < \tau-\delta$ and engaged, with a small dead-band $\delta$. The
combination of an angle \emph{threshold} and a press \emph{speed/depth} gain is
the system's distinguishing control feature (patented; see
Section~\ref{sec:ip}).

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/fig_control.pdf}
\caption{Head-angle control. (a) The transmitted value maps the head angle above
a calibrated neutral, scaled by a user gain and clamped. (b) Engage/release use
hysteresis with a small dead-band to avoid chatter near the threshold.}
\label{fig:control}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Rate decoupling (key insight).}
AR face tracking produces a new head angle every camera frame ($\sim$60\,fps),
but the BLE link cannot reliably carry that rate. Rather than transmit per frame,
the tracker only \emph{stores} the latest angle into a shared variable; a separate
timer samples and sends it at $\sim$10\,Hz (a $100$\,ms period), and the discrete
engage/release events are throttled to a $\ge 10$\,ms minimum interval. In early
development the send period was as low as $5$\,ms and produced communication
faults; raising it to $100$\,ms made the link robust. Thus the fast sensor and the
slow radio meet through a single shared variable, decoupling the two timing
domains---conceptually mirrored on the device side, where an event-driven BLE
stack feeds a synchronous motion loop over a wired UART.

\section{Pro device: robotic actuation for acoustic pianos}
The Pro device places a small, 3D-printed, aluminium-extrusion-framed unit at the
piano's pedal lyre. A motor drives a timing belt and a lead screw that advances a
push-rod onto the sustain pedal, so the transmitted value $v$ maps linearly to
pedal depth between calibrated travel limits (Figure~\ref{fig:pro}).

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{figures/fig_pro_mechanism.pdf}
\caption{Pro device. A motor drives a timing belt and lead screw that advances a
push-rod onto the sustain pedal; an inflatable airback beneath a neighbouring
pedal absorbs the reaction force and anchors the unit on an unmodified piano.}
\label{fig:pro}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Airback reaction-force anchoring.}
Pressing the pedal produces a reaction force that would otherwise push the
lightweight unit away. We absorb this force with a pneumatic \emph{airback}: an
inflatable bag is placed beneath a neighbouring pedal and inflated by a small
pump at setup, clamping the unit in place. This anchors the device on an
\emph{unmodified} piano without screws or adhesives---a practical requirement for
shared and valuable instruments.

\paragraph{Sensorless limit calibration.}
At power-up the firmware (i) runs the air pump until a pressure threshold confirms
anchoring, then (ii) drives the rod toward the pedal until the \emph{motor current}
(proportional to reaction torque) rises at the hard stop, fixing the
\emph{lower} travel limit; the \emph{upper} limit is set live by a slider on a
hand controller. No limit switches are required.

\section{Switch device: electronic actuation for digital instruments}
For electric pianos and keyboards, the Switch realization needs no motor: the BLE
board drives a digital output (with a relay/opto element) that opens and closes
the instrument's \emph{sustain-pedal jack}, acting as the pedal switch. An
on-board RGB LED indicates the engage state. Because polarity conventions differ
across manufacturers, an on/off-type setting inverts the output sense.

\section{Implementation}
The smartphone controller is a SwiftUI iOS application using ARKit for face
tracking and CoreBluetooth for the BLE link; it streams the value $v$ with
fire-and-forget writes, which are safe precisely because the application
rate-limits itself (Section~\ref{sec:control}). The pedal device uses an
nRF52840 BLE board (Adafruit Bluefruit / Nordic UART~\cite{nus}) for the radio;
the Pro main board is an RP2040 (Raspberry Pi Pico)~\cite{rp2040} that drives the
motor, the air pump (via a MOSFET), and the sensors. The wireless protocol is a
small ASCII vocabulary (streamed values plus engage/release and configuration
bytes). The complete iOS source, firmware, mechanical (CAD/3D-print) designs, and
documentation are released as open source~\cite{repo}.

\section{Deployment and evaluation}
bFaaaP has been used in public settings since 2018, including piano recitals,
university festivals, an international assistive-equipment exhibition, and
full concerts (2024 and 2025). In these settings, players who cannot use the foot
pedal---including a wheelchair user---performed pieces requiring sustain. This
paper reports a deployment/feasibility evaluation; a controlled user study with
quantitative latency, accuracy, and usability metrics is planned future work.

\section{Discussion}
The approach generalizes beyond the sustain pedal: the same head-angle channel
could drive other expressive controls or other instruments. Limitations include
dependence on a clear view of the player's face, per-instrument calibration, and
the current lack of quantitative user-study data. The Pro mechanism's motor
choice is also evolving (an earlier smart-servo line was discontinued, motivating
a closed-loop stepper), which the open hardware documentation tracks.

\section{Intellectual property and openness}
\label{sec:ip}
The two-parameter pedal-control method (a head-angle threshold and the press
speed/preference) is protected by two patents currently in force in Japan
(JP~6726319~B2, ``Auxiliary pedal system''; JP~7004771~B2, ``Device controller''),
with an English PCT application as the priority basis (WO~2019/176164).
\emph{bFaaaP} is a registered trademark and the glasses-mounted motion sensor used
in early prototypes is a registered design. The project is nonetheless released as
open source to maximize reach. Licensing of the patents generally depends on the
intended embodiment; however, \textbf{for uses with a genuinely public, open
purpose the authors intend to grant a licence free of charge} (please contact the
corresponding author). For software reuse, a license with an explicit patent grant
is recommended.

\section{Conclusion}
bFaaaP turns a small head movement into expressive piano-pedal control, realized
both as a non-destructive robotic actuator for acoustic pianos and as an
electronic switch for digital instruments, from a single smartphone controller.
By open-sourcing the full system we hope to make pedal-based piano performance
\emph{inclusive}---available to anyone, regardless of whether they can use a foot.
